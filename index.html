01

MEP PE Deal Activity

Truelink Capital forms a mission-critical MEP platform with Horwitz

In June, Los Angeles-based Truelink Capital made a significant investment in Horwitz, a 1918-founded Minneapolis provider of mechanical, electrical, plumbing, and building-automation services with roughly 720 employees, including 540 technicians. The deal establishes Horwitz as a regional platform serving high-growth, mission-critical end markets — data centers, semiconductor fabrication, medtech, and healthcare — anchored by a recurring service-and-maintenance base. For Truelink, this is a platform formation rather than a bolt-on: a foundation for consolidating adjacent MEP capabilities across the Upper Midwest. The transaction reflects a broader pattern in commercial MEP, where sponsors increasingly favor multi-trade contractors able to self-perform across disciplines on complex facilities rather than single-trade specialists.

02

Market / Strategy Insight

Commercial MEP is still early in its consolidation cycle

While residential HVAC consolidation is widely viewed as roughly midway through its cycle, commercial MEP services remain in the early innings, according to PKF Investment Banking — a gap that helps explain where sponsor attention is shifting. Commercial platforms command premium multiples to residential roll-ups, supported by recurring maintenance revenue, multi-trade bundling, and exposure to data-center and mission-critical demand. Deal volume cooled in early 2026 — R.L. Hulett tracked a 21.7% sequential decline in Q1 MEP transactions amid higher project costs and labor constraints — yet private equity's share of that volume reached a four-year high, underscoring sustained sponsor appetite. The pattern points to a longer runway for commercial MEP consolidation than headline volume alone suggests.

03

FMI Thought Leadership

PE's playbook shifts from financial engineering to operational execution

In FMI's latest “Inside FMI” Q&A, financial-sponsor-coverage lead Jayson Post argues that private equity's value-creation model in the built environment is changing: with multiple expansion and financial engineering harder to rely on, returns increasingly hinge on operational execution, fueling more operator-led strategies. He points to the electrical value chain — from power generation through in-facility electrical infrastructure — as the most active investment area, driven by electrification, AI-led data-center demand, and aging systems. Post notes that platform buyers prize market tailwinds, durable revenue, and fragmentation suited to buy-and-build — but cautions that acquirers at exit now expect demonstrated M&A integration, not merely deal count. Capital, he expects, will keep concentrating in the lower-middle market.

04

Runding Case Study

AiRCO Mechanical — building the operational foundation for scale

AiRCO Mechanical, a Central Texas HVAC, electrical, and plumbing contractor, has partnered with Runding to strengthen its operational foundation during a period of rapid growth. As volume scaled, AiRCO encountered familiar specialty-contractor pressure points: limited visibility into operational performance, harder capacity planning, and tighter margin protection across an expanding project portfolio. The engagement deploys OpenBuild to centralize project tracking, improve financial visibility, and structure workflows around AiRCO's existing project lifecycle — augmenting current systems rather than replacing them, so field teams retain the flexibility they depend on. The engagement reflects a recurring reality in MEP consolidation: rapid growth exposes the gap between field execution and financial control, and durable scale rests on the project-controls infrastructure that protects margin.

Signal Over Volume — 5 Deals

The transactions that best read where the commercial MEP roll-up market is heading.

1

Blackstone → AIR Control Concepts

HVAC / Electrical|Recapitalization|~1,900 emp.

Blackstone bought out Madison Dearborn to become sole institutional owner of the largest commercial HVAC, electrical, and controls platform in North America — and parked it in BXPE, its perpetual-capital vehicle, signaling a long hold. The clearest sign yet that mega-cap capital treats commercial MEP, with its data-center and mission-critical exposure, as a core consolidation theme rather than an adjacency.

2

Truelink Capital → Horwitz

HVAC / Electrical / Plumbing|Platform formation|~720 emp.

Truelink formed a platform around a 1918-founded, mission-critical multi-trade MEP provider serving data centers, semiconductor fabs, and healthcare. It is a clean expression of the convergence thesis: sponsors increasingly want one platform that can self-perform across all the MEP trades on high-complexity facilities.

3

Bain Capital · Mubadala → Service Logic

HVAC / Mechanical|Recap

Bain Capital and Abu Dhabi's Mubadala took control of Service Logic — widely cited as the largest commercial mechanical services platform — from Leonard Green. Sovereign capital entering U.S. commercial MEP at this scale underscores how much runway sponsors see in a commercial services cycle still in its early innings.

4

Ares Management → Techpro Power Group

Electrical · Power|Buyout / Platform · ~$350M

Ares, with Energy Impact Partners and Integrated Power Services, backed a ~$350M buyout of an electrical testing and commissioning provider for power and industrial systems. Power reliability is the gating constraint on the data-center build-out, which turns commissioning capacity into a strategically scarce asset rather than a commodity trade.

5

Legence → Bowers

Mechanical|Add-on · ~$475M

Legence, the Blackstone-backed energy-services and mechanical platform, opened the year with a ~$475M add-on of Bowers, a mechanical contractor serving commercial and institutional facilities. It shows scaled platforms continuing capability-deepening M&A even as headline volume softened, and the convergence of mechanical contracting with energy-performance services.

Scope & method. The tracker covers U.S. HVAC, mechanical, plumbing, electrical, and fire & life safety contractors with private-equity involvement (platform, add-on, or recap), dated January 1, 2025 – June 22, 2026, commercial end markets only. Pure component manufacturers, distributors, and building-automation product makers are out of scope this cycle. Employee counts are shown only where explicitly stated by a credible source — never estimated; a dash (—) means undisclosed.
